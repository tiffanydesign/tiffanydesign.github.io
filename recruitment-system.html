02 · Problem Space
What Was Actually Broken

Before designing anything, I spent time with the HR team watching how they actually worked. The problems weren't abstract process inefficiencies. They were specific, daily friction points that recruiters had quietly accepted as normal.

Every Stage Required a Manual Push

HR triggered each pipeline step by hand: moving candidates forward, sending notifications, marking rejections. With 100+ open roles at once, things fell through the cracks. Hiring timelines stretched not because the process was slow, but because someone had to remember to advance it.

Candidates Left While HR Was Still Waiting

Hiring managers were deep in projects, and interview feedback wasn't their top priority. Without automatic reminders, feedback sat incomplete for days. By the time HR followed up, strong candidates had accepted other offers. Crucial info was scattered across group chats, making it hard to track.

Coordination Was Eating HR's Entire Day

A panel interview involving tech, product, and business teams meant manually checking 3–5 people's calendars, confirming through group chat, and then chasing them again for feedback afterward. This coordination overhead was eating up most of HR's workday.

Solution 1 of 3

Recruitment Automation

Turn manual pushes into system-driven flows

Automated Recruitment Flows

  • HR sets up trigger rules once: when a candidate passes screening, the system advances them to the next stage, notifies all relevant parties, and queues the next action automatically. Pass moves forward. Fail sends a rejection. Nobody has to chase anyone.

Unified Channel Management

  • Previously, HR logged into LinkedIn, Indeed, internal referrals, and campus platforms separately to refresh listings and download resumes. Now it's one action: publish everywhere, receive everything in a single inbox.

Smart Screening & Deduplication

  • With 100+ resumes arriving per role per day, the first problem is volume. Smart tagging flags candidates who don't meet hard requirements so HR can focus on the qualified ones without reading everything. Cross-channel deduplication catches when the same person applied through multiple platforms, preventing duplicate outreach.
Solution 2 of 3

Panel Interview Collaboration

Automate scheduling and feedback collection

Smart Interview Scheduling

  • Scheduling a three-person panel meant HR manually checking three calendars, negotiating times through group chat, and chasing each interviewer for feedback afterward. The coordination around an interview took more effort than the interview itself.
  • The system reads all interviewers' calendar availability and proposes valid time slots automatically. Once confirmed, it sends calendar invites to everyone, candidate, interviewers, and HR, with all details included. After the interview ends, a reminder fires automatically with a direct link to the feedback form.

Flexible Interview Formats

  • Different roles need very different interview structures. A designer might go through portfolio review, a design assignment, and two panel rounds; an engineer goes through a technical screen and an onsite. HR configures the full interview sequence per role type upfront, instead of improvising it for every candidate.

Structured Candidate Evaluation

  • Every interviewer sees the same evaluation dimensions for a given role, no freeform notes that are impossible to compare across candidates. Scores and comments are collected in one place, visible to the full panel after everyone submits. HR gets a consolidated view of all evaluations the moment the last interviewer submits, so the decision can happen the same day instead of waiting for follow-up messages.
Solution 3 of 3

Talent Pool & Analytics

Turn short-term hiring into long-term talent assets

Talent Pool Management

  • A strong candidate wasn't hired this round. Three months later, a similar role opens — but nobody remembers who was interviewed before. HR starts sourcing from scratch.
  • Candidates who weren't hired but received positive evaluations are automatically added to a searchable pool, with all past assessment notes preserved. When a similar position opens later, HR can surface and re-engage them in one click.

Data Analytics & Visibility

  • The dashboard answers the questions HR actually asks: where are candidates dropping off? Which departments have the slowest feedback turnaround? Which channels produce the best offer acceptance rates?
  • All data is logged automatically — no manual report compilation required.
04 · Iteration
What Changed Along the Way
01

Configuration Mode: from "Full Customization" to "Template + Custom" Dual Mode

The original automation flow builder gave HR full control over every step — node by node, rule by rule. In testing, users stalled. The blank canvas felt overwhelming, not empowering. I restructured it into two modes: start from a preset template (covering the most common hiring flows), or build from scratch. Most users defaulted to templates and only customized the parts that differed. The barrier dropped, and average setup time fell from 15 minutes to under 3 minutes.

02

Low Feedback Submission Rate → Automatic Reminders with Direct Links

Post-launch data showed interview feedback submission was stuck below target. Interviewers weren't skipping on purpose — they were simply forgetting, or couldn't find the form when they did remember. I added a timed automatic reminder sent after each interview, with a deep link that opened directly to that candidate's feedback form. No searching, no extra steps. Submission rate improved by 60%.

Reflection
What I Learned & How I've Grown

On working solo

As the sole designer, I learned to make decisions without anyone reviewing my work. That meant building my own checkpoints — writing out the reasoning behind each choice, pressure-testing it against edge cases, and being willing to reverse course when the logic didn't hold. The absence of a design team wasn't a gap to compensate for; it was a forcing function for cleaner thinking.

On automation

My initial instinct was to automate everything possible. But during testing, HR pushed back: "What if the system automatically rejects someone I wanted to keep?" The final design kept human confirmation at critical decision points. Automation handled the repetitive pushing and notifying, not the judgment calls. The lesson: automation should reduce effort, not override judgment.
